Executive Summary
Recent advances in multivariate time series forecasting are dominated by deep learning models and large "foundation" models. Transformer-based approaches now often match or beat classical VAR/ARIMA baselines. For example, AWS Chronos (a T5-style pretrained language model for time series) and Salesforce Moirai (a universal forecasting Transformer) excel at zero-shot forecasting across diverse datasets. Specialized convolutional models (e.g. FourCastNet) and graph neural nets (e.g. GraphCast) have achieved weather-forecast accuracy comparable to traditional NWP models at a fraction of the computational cost. Hybrid physics-ML schemes (e.g. FengWu-4DVar, XiChen) are emerging to integrate learning with 4D-Var data assimilation, aiming for fully AI-driven forecasts without reliance on legacy models. These innovations come with PyTorch-native libraries (Chronos, PyTorch Forecasting, NeuralOperator, etc.) that support GPUs/TPUs. In this report we survey the SOTA model families (2023-2026), emphasize weather/climate forecasting applications, list key PyTorch repos, and compare methods on metrics like multivariate scaling, spatial resolution, lead time, uncertainty quantification (UQ), interpretability, data and compute needs. We recommend models for scenarios (nowcasting, medium-range, climate signals, downscaling, assimilation) and note standard datasets/benchmarks (ERA5/WeatherBench, M4/M5, etc.) and evaluation metrics (RMSE, anomaly correlation, CRPS) used in the field. A timeline of major advances (2020-2026) and diagrams illustrate model architectures.

1. Model Families in Multivariate Forecasting
- Transformers & LLMs: Transformer-based forecasters (e.g. Informer, Autoformer, PatchTST) remain state-of-the-art for long-range multivariate series. In 2023-2026, "foundation models" appeared: e.g. AWS Chronos/Chronos-2 (T5-style pretrained on thousands of series) and Salesforce Moirai (decoder-only transformer) achieve strong in-dataset and zero-shot performance. Chronos tokenizes and quantizes series to train a language model, yielding top accuracy on many benchmarks and supporting probabilistic forecasts; Chronos-2 adds covariates and patching (Chronos-Bolt is a speed-optimized variant). Lag-Llama (2023) similarly uses a pretrained Transformer (decoder-only) for univariate probabilistic forecasting. Typical advantages: capture long-range dependencies, easy multivariate input, built-in attention giving some interpretability. Downsides include heavy data/computation needs and difficulty quantifying uncertainty without ensembling or built-in probabilistic heads.
- Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs) and CNNs: remain popular for short-term and nowcasting tasks. Models like N-BEATS and 1D-CNNs (TCN) give fast inference and handle multivariate inputs with moderate data. In weather/climate, 2D/3D CNNs dominate. CNNs are less expensive than Transformers and easier to parallelize but struggle with very long-range dependencies. FourCastNet uses Fourier layers and sparse convolutions to capture spatial patterns.
- Recurrent Networks (RNNs): Classic LSTM/GRU models (e.g. DeepAR) were once popular for multivariate forecasting, but have largely been surpassed by TCNs and Transformers. They handle variable-length sequences and provide natural UQ via state-space formulations (e.g. DeepState combines RNNs with Kalman filters), but training can be harder (vanishing gradients) and they scale poorly in parallel.
- N-BEATS / Legacy MLP: N-BEATS (Oreshkin et al. 2020) introduced interpretable basis-expansion blocks for univariate forecasting, extended by N-BEATSx with exogenous inputs. Strengths: interpretability, relatively low data requirements. Weakness: fixed univariate output, not spatial.
- Probabilistic SSMs (DeepAR/DeepState): DeepAR (Salinas et al. 2017/2020) and related deep state-space models use RNNs to model distributions, handling multivariate settings by training one model across series, outputting probabilistic forecasts via sampling. Require many similar series for best performance.
- Gaussian Processes (GPs): offer principled uncertainty, but traditional GPs scale poorly (O(N^3)). GPyTorch (MIT license) provides GPU-accelerated GPs, scalable with modern GPU hardware. Sparse and multi-output GP variants can handle some multivariate forecasting. GP strengths: explicit uncertainty, works well with limited data, interpretable kernels. Weakness: scales to only medium data, struggles with high-dimensional inputs.
- Neural ODE / Continuous-time models: (e.g. torchdiffeq, torchdyn) can model continuous-time series, shine on irregular sampling and integrating physical ODE knowledge, but require strong inductive biases. Niche for generic forecasting.
- Spatio-Temporal Graph Neural Nets: popular for multivariate time series on irregular grids or networks (traffic: DCRNN, Graph WaveNet, MTGNN; climate: icosahedral-mesh GNNs). GraphCast (Lam et al. 2023) uses a multi-resolution icosahedral GNN to forecast global weather on a sphere, setting a new standard in medium-range (10-day) forecasting. Strengths: capture spatial dependencies, handle multivariate grid data. Weaknesses: complex to implement, need careful graph design, memory-heavy for high resolutions.
- Graph Neural PDE Solvers (Neural Operators): Fourier Neural Operator (FNO), DeepONet, etc. learn mappings between function spaces rather than finite-dimensional vectors. NeuralOperator (MIT license, PyTorch) implements FNO and variants, used for fluid and climate modeling. Strength: resolution-invariance, incorporate physical PDE structure. Weakness: requires gridded data, heavy compute for high resolution.
- Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs): embed PDE constraints into the loss (Raissi et al., 2019). Active research for climate subgrid physics/parameterizations, not mainstream in operational forecasting. Strengths: incorporate known physics, need less data if physics is strong. Weaknesses: unstable training, slower, not straightforward for noisy observational data.
- Ensemble/Hybrid Models: state-of-art systems increasingly use ensembles (multiple ML models or MC dropout) to quantify uncertainty. Hybrid approaches combine ML with classical NWP (FengWu-4DVar, AIFS, XiChen). On the frontier in weather forecasting.

Key Advances (2023-2026): Foundation Models (Chronos, Moirai, Lag-Llama); Spatial-Temporal ML for Weather (FourCastNet, GraphCast, Pangu-Weather, FuXi, FengWu); Assimilation & End-to-End Systems (FengWu-4DVar, XiChen, ECMWF's AIFS/Anemoi).

2. Models in Weather, Climate, and Earth-System Forecasting
FourCastNet (2022): CNN-based, adaptive Fourier layers, global forecasts at 3-hour resolution. GraphCast (2023): GNN on multi-resolution icosahedral meshes, outperformed operational deterministic systems on 90% of 1380 targets in 10-day forecasts. Pangu-Weather (2023): 3D spherical CNNs with Earth-specific biases, outperforms ECMWF HRES on reanalysis. Transformers/diffusion entering climate modeling (Stormer, HEAL-ViT, SwinRDM). ECMWF's Anemoi (2024) provides data pipelines/training/inference/graph tools; AIFS runs a 28km grid ML forecast daily. Downscaling/super-resolution via CNNs, GANs, diffusion models. Datasets: ERA5, WeatherBench, CMIP, Copernicus products.

3. PyTorch Implementations, Libraries, and Repos
Chronos (AWS, Apache-2.0): PyTorch framework for forecasting with language models, Chronos2Pipeline API. uni2ts/Moirai (Salesforce, Apache-2.0): universal Transformer forecasters with Any-Variate Attention. TorchTS, PyTorch-Forecasting (sktime, MIT): N-BEATS, TFT, TCN with PyTorch Lightning backend. GPyTorch (MIT): GPU-accelerated GPs. NeuralOperator (MIT): FNO and neural PDE models. Torchdiffeq/Torchdyn: Neural ODEs/SDEs. PyTorch Geometric/DGL: GNN layers. Anemoi packages (ECMWF, Apache-2.0): datasets/training/models/graphs for weather ML.

4. Comparison of Model Families
Transformers/LLMs: fully multivariate, excellent for large-scale patterns, very high compute cost. CNN/TCN: local multivariate, good for nowcasting/downscaling, moderate compute. RNN/LSTM: sequential, historically used in demand/finance, low-moderate compute, less used for spatial data. N-BEATS: usually univariate, high interpretability, low compute, not for spatial climate data. DeepAR/DeepState: many-series probabilistic, used in retail demand. Gaussian Processes: strong Bayesian UQ, low scalability (cubic), niche for scarce-sensor Earth systems. Neural ODEs: good for physical ODE-governed processes, rare in mainstream weather forecasting. Spatio-Temporal GNNs: excellent for Earth models needing irregular meshes. Neural Operators: best for fluid/atmosphere dynamics on regular grids, less interpretable. PINNs: potential for climate sub-grid physics, still research-phase. Ensembles/Hybrid: essential for operational forecasting.

5. Recommendations by Use Case
- Short-term nowcasting (0-6h, high-res): CNN-based models (MetNet-4, ConvLSTM, U-Net).
- Medium-range (1-10 days): Transformer/GNN models (GraphCast, Pangu-Weather, FuXi); PyTorch Geometric for multi-mesh GNNs, or TemporalFusionTransformer for sequence-flattened reanalysis grids.
- Long-range/climate signals (>10 days to seasonal): simpler/hybrid models (ensemble persistence, climatology, linear state-space) often match SOTA; GPs or regularized RNNs for low-frequency variability; GPyTorch with Matern kernel for seasonal patterns.
- Downscaling & super-resolution: deep CNNs or GANs (DeepSD, ESRGAN-style); torch.nn.ConvTranspose2d/upsampling; NeuralOperator for low-res to high-res mapping.
- Data assimilation: FengWu-4DVar/XiChen embed assimilation; differentiable 4D-Var via autodiff (PyTorch backprop through forecast+observation loss).

6. Benchmarks and Metrics
Datasets: ERA5 reanalysis, WeatherBench, ECMWF HRES runs, M4/M5 (general time series), CMIP outputs, reforecast hindcasts, Anemoi-Datasets. Metrics: RMSE/MSE, Anomaly Correlation Coefficient (ACC) for deterministic forecasts (e.g. 500hPa geopotential); CRPS and spread-skill diagnostics for probabilistic/ensemble forecasts; MAE, bias, rank histograms, energy score for spatial fields. Standard practice: cross-validation on hold-out years (e.g. 1979-2018 train / 2019-2022 test on ERA5), fixed random seeds, proper climatological baselines.

7. Timeline of Major Advances
Early: N-BEATS (2020), Fourier Neural Operator (2021). Middle: FourCastNet (2022), GraphCast/Pangu/FuXi (2023). Recent: Foundation models (Chronos, Moirai, Lag-Llama), hybrid assimilation (FengWu-4DVar, XiChen) (2024-2026).

8. Conclusion
The state of multivariate forecasting has rapidly shifted toward deep learning and "foundation" models. PyTorch now hosts a rich ecosystem (Chronos, PyTorch Forecasting, NeuralOperator, etc.) to implement Transformers, CNNs, GNNs, GPs, and physics-informed networks. These models offer superior performance on complex datasets (especially spatially rich earth-system data) but require careful handling of UQ, data scale, and compute. Match model to task: CNNs/GNNs for spatial forecasting (e.g. weather), Transformers for general multivariate sequences (leveraging pretrained LMs where applicable), and ensembles/hybrids for assimilation-heavy applications. Operational adoption is accelerating (ECMWF's Anemoi provides production-ready code).

Executive Summary
Recent progress (2023–2026) in multivariate time-series forecasting has yielded powerful new models and tools. Leading architectures include Transformer-based variants and MLP/linear methods. For example, Crossformer (ICLR’23) embeds the series into a 2D (time × feature) representation and applies a “two-stage” attention to capture both cross-time and cross-variable dependencies. iTransformer (2023) “inverts” the axes: it treats each series (variable) as a token and attends across series, achieving state-of-the-art results and efficient long-window learning. BasisFormer (NeurIPS’23) learns a set of latent basis series via self-supervised (contrastive) pretraining and uses cross-attention to compose forecasts; it reported ~11–16% error reduction over prior SOTA. Multi-scale Transformers have also excelled: Pathformer (ICLR’24) and MultiPatchFormer (2025) divide inputs into patches at multiple resolutions and combine global and local attention. MultiPatchFormer’s channel-wise encoder and multi-step decoder yield SOTA errors on 7 real datasets. Informer-inspired models like TACDformer (Sci. Rep. 2026) add multi-scale feature distillation and an encoder-only design; TACDformer outperforms 8 baselines (including TimeMixer and iTransformer) on 7 benchmarks while greatly reducing computational cost.

Simplicity also works: MLP or linear models (e.g. TimeMixer 2024) mix decomposed multiscale inputs and ensemble predictors to reach new high accuracy on both short- and long-term tasks. Linear autoregression variants (N-Linear, D-Linear) have been shown to beat Transformers on many benchmarks, highlighting that complex attention is not always needed. Overall, recent papers report large gains over older models: e.g. on the Traffic (862-var) benchmark iTransformer achieved MSE=0.178 and MAE=0.270 (96h ahead) vs 0.193/0.293 for a standard Transformer. PatchTST (ICLR’23) similarly reported ~21.0% lower MSE than prior Transformers.

PyTorch-native libraries now provide these models and more. PyTorch Forecasting (Lightning-based) offers a high-level API with a TimeSeriesDataSet class and built-in models (TFT, N-BEATS, DeepAR, etc.) for time series. Darts (Nixtla) integrates 100+ classical and deep models under a unified fit()/predict() interface, supporting multivariate data, covariates, ensembles, and probabilistic outputs. NeuralForecast (Nixtla) implements 30+ global forecasting architectures (LSTM/TCN, DeepAR, TimesNet, BiTCN, N-BEATS, N-HITS, TS-Mixer, DLinear/NLinear, TFT, Informer, PatchTST, iTransformer, etc.) with easy batching and covariates. These frameworks support GPUs/TPUs, multi-GPU, and quantile/regression losses for uncertainty. They often include data-preprocessing pipelines (scaling, encoding, handling missing data) and tools for hyperparameter tuning and backtesting.

In practice, researchers and engineers can leverage these libraries and model repos to reproduce results. For example, Crossformer and iTransformer have official GitHub releases with training scripts. Common recipes use Adam optimizer (lr~1e-3), batch sizes 32–128, and early stopping. PyTorch Forecasting’s example code (below) shows a typical pattern: define a TimeSeriesDataSet, convert to dataloaders, then instantiate and train a TemporalFusionTransformer in a few lines.

Deployment considerations favor encoder-only or patch models for speed: iTransformer and TACDformer train much faster than standard Transformers on high-dimensional data. Multi-step forecasting (direct output of all future steps) is standard now, avoiding autoregressive slowdowns. Uncertainty quantification (via quantile losses or conformal methods) is supported by these frameworks. Overall, modern PyTorch tools make it straightforward to train SOTA MTS models at scale.

Below is a detailed survey of these models, libraries, implementation resources, benchmark comparisons, and recommendations.

1. SOTA Academic Models (2023–2026)
Crossformer (ICLR’23): Explicitly models cross-variable and cross-time attention. It embeds the multivariate series into a 2D grid (Dimension-Segment-Wise embedding) to preserve time and feature info, then applies a Two-Stage Attention: one layer attends across time for each variable, the next attends across variables for each time. A hierarchical encoder-decoder captures multi-scale patterns. On 6 datasets (electricity, traffic, etc.), Crossformer significantly outperformed prior Transformers. Its GitHub provides training scripts for Electricity, Traffic, etc.

iTransformer (2023): Flips the standard axes: each time series (variable) becomes a token. In the encoder, self-attention operates over the set of variables at each time step, capturing cross-series correlations directly, while each variable’s own time dimension is handled by a feed-forward network. The result is state-of-the-art accuracy on benchmarks like ETT, Traffic, Solar-Energy. Notably, iTransformer also exhibits better training speed: when variate count is large (e.g. Traffic, Weather), iTransformer trains faster than the vanilla Transformer with similar memory use. (It’s implemented with PyTorch Lightning; code is available.) For example, on the Traffic-96h task, iTransformer’s MSE=0.178 vs 0.193 for the baseline, MAE=0.270 vs 0.293, a clear improvement.

BasisFormer (NeurIPS’23): Learns a set of basis time series (global patterns) via contrastive self-supervised learning. The model has three parts: (1) A Bases block learns K latent series by contrasting past/future segments; (2) A Coef module uses cross-attention between the input series and the bases to compute weights; (3) A Forecast module combines the selected bases to predict future values. On six standard MTS datasets, BasisFormer reduced errors by ~11.04% (univariate) and 15.78% (multivariate) relative to previous SOTA. Its learnable bases provide interpretability (one can inspect which basis patterns are used).

Multi-Scale Transformers:

PatchTST (ICLR’23): Segments (patches) of length P are created from each series, then fed into a Transformer. It also uses channel-independence (each variable uses the same weights). PatchTST showed that a Transformer with patching and long lookbacks beats many alternatives: e.g. on ETT/Traffic it achieved 21.0% lower MSE and 16.7% lower MAE than the best prior Transformer models. PatchTST is implemented in PyTorch with examples in Darts and PyTorch Forecasting now.
Pathformer (ICLR’24): Extends the idea of multi-scale patching. It constructs patches of different sizes (e.g. daily, weekly, monthly) and uses a dual attention mechanism: one attention aggregates global context across all scales, and a second attention captures local details. Crucially, adaptive pathways let the model select relevant scales per input. On 11 datasets spanning traffic, energy, etc., Pathformer achieved new SOTA (best-in-class) performance, and showed strong transfer to new tasks.
MultiPatchFormer (2025, Sci. Rep.): Another multi-scale approach: time series are divided into patches at various resolutions. A temporal encoder processes patches of each scale; then a channel-wise encoder models inter-variable interactions. Finally, a multi-step linear decoder generates all forecast steps. This avoids a single giant output layer and reduces noise. Experiments on seven real datasets (Electricity, Traffic, Weather, Solar-Energy, Exchange, etc.) found that MultiPatchFormer achieves state-of-the-art errors, outperforming all baselines. For example, on Traffic (862 vars) it beats the next-best model by >5% MSE (7.7% MAE).
Informer Variants (Long-Range Forecasting): Informer (2021) introduced sparse self-attention and distillation for long sequences. Recent work (including TACDformer) builds on this. TACDformer (Apr 2026) uses Informer’s sparse attention but adds: (1) a multi-scale feature distillation layer (extracting local patterns at multiple scales); (2) an encoder-only design (no traditional decoder); (3) an output layer with separate temporal and channel attention. It also applies reversible normalization to stabilize distribution shifts. On seven benchmarks (electricity, energy, finance, weather), TACDformer outperformed eight baselines (including TimeMixer and iTransformer) in MAE/MSE. Compared to the original Informer, it reduced model complexity by 33% and memory use by 63%, cutting runtime by ~77%.

MLP and Linear Models:

TimeMixer (2024): A purely feed-forward model. It decomposes the history into multiscale components (fine/coarse) with Past-Decomposable Mixing, then Future-Mixer blocks ensemble predictors. This architecture explicitly disentangles seasonal and trend at multiple scales. TimeMixer achieved consistent SOTA performance on both long- and short-term forecasting (where Transformers falter) with high efficiency.
N-Linear / D-Linear (2023): Recent studies found that extremely simple models (one linear layer per series) can match or beat many deep models on standard benchmarks. These models act as baselines: D-Linear (feed-forward linear) and N-Linear (each variate has independent linear regression) have often shown surprisingly strong results, especially on data with mostly linear trends.
Other Notables: Some older models still matter as baselines (e.g. N-BEATS, WaveNet, TCN), and domain-specific methods (ConvLSTM for spatiotemporal data) exist. But the newest state-of-the-art papers focus on the above architectures.

2. PyTorch-Native Libraries & Frameworks
PyTorch Forecasting (Lightning-based): An end-to-end library for time-series forecasting on tabular data. It provides:

High-Level API: A TimeSeriesDataSet class handles formatting of data (with time index, group IDs, static and time-varying covariates). It supports missing data, standard scalers, and automatic normalization.
Built-in Models: Implementations of many popular architectures: Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT), RecurrentNetworks (DeepAR-like), N-BEATS, N-HiTS, DeepAR, Multi-Horizon MLP, etc.
Training Utilities: Leveraging PyTorch Lightning, it offers built-in early stopping, LR scheduling, TensorBoard logging, and easy multi-GPU/TPU support.
Prediction & Losses: Supports point and probabilistic forecasts (quantile/multi-quantile loss), backtesting, and interpretability (SHAP integration).

Darts (Unit8/Nixtla): A unified Python library for forecasting (and anomaly detection). Darts models range from ARIMA/ExponentialSmoothing to modern deep nets, all with a simple API. Global Models: Darts's RNNModel (supports LSTM/GRU) can be trained on a list of series jointly (like global DeepAR). N-BEATS, N-HiTS, TCN, and TransformerModel are also available. Non-Deep Models: ARIMA, Theta, Exponential Smoothing, and the statsforecast library can be used via Darts. Probabilistic Forecasts: Many models can output probabilistic forecasts by fitting a distribution. Darts also provides ConformalQuantile regression wrappers.

NeuralForecast (Nixtlaverse): A newer global-forecasting library from the NeuralForecast team. It integrates ~30+ pretrained architectures for multivariate forecasting, including LSTM/GRU (DeepAR), TCN, TimesNet, BiTCN, StemGNN, TS-Mixer, NBEATS, NBEATSx, N-HITS, TiDE, TimeMixer, DLinear, NLinear, TFT, Informer, AutoFormer, FedFormer, PatchTST, iTransformer, TimeLLM, and more.

3. Practical Implementations: Code, Checkpoints, and Reproducibility
Modern MTS research strongly emphasizes reproducibility. Key papers almost universally provide code and data links. Model Repositories: For nearly every new model, authors have released GitHub code (Crossformer, iTransformer, BasisFormer, Pathformer, TACDformer). Hyperparameters & Recipes: Papers typically list model hyperparameters (layers, heads, hidden dims, learning rate, weight decay). PyTorch Forecasting and Darts libraries also suggest reasonable defaults (e.g. hidden sizes 16–64, learning rate ~1e-3).

4. Benchmark Comparisons
Standard Datasets: Electricity (ETT/ETTh1, ETTm1, etc.), Traffic, Solar-Energy, Weather, ExchangeRate. Reported results show iTransformer achieving MSE=0.178/MAE=0.270 on Traffic-96h vs 0.193/0.293 for a standard Transformer; on Weather-96h, MSE=0.428/MAE=0.282 vs 0.913/0.500. PatchTST reported ~21.0% MSE reduction vs prior SOTA. BasisFormer reported ~15.78% overall error reduction (multivariate). TACDformer vs Informer: complexity −33%, memory −63%, time −77%.

5. Deployment & Scaling Considerations
Inference Speed & Memory: Encoder-only or patch-based models (PatchTST, Pathformer, iTransformer, TACDformer) are faster at inference because they predict the entire horizon in parallel. Model size also matters: e.g. TACDformer cuts parameter count vs Informer by 33%. Multi-Step Forecasting: Nearly all modern models output a fixed-size window of future values directly via a linear projection on the final hidden state(s), avoiding step-by-step decoding. Quantile/Uncertainty: Frameworks support quantile forecasting natively.

6. Open Problems & Recommendations
Irregular/Missing Data: Very few deep learning models directly handle irregular timestamps or missing entries natively. Exogenous Variables: Models like TFT, DeepAR, or NeuralForecast's models can include static or time-varying known covariates. Long-Horizon Stability: Models tend to degrade for very long horizons because noise accumulates; strategies include increasing receptive field, multi-scale architectures, or hierarchical prediction.

Model Selection by Use Case:
- Short-term, high-frequency: fast models (TCN, small CNNs) or a small Transformer (PatchTST) if cross-series correlations matter.
- Long-term forecasting: multi-scale/decomposed models (Autoformer, TACDformer, Pathformer); consider ensembling multiple models.
- Large numbers of series: global training (NeuralForecast, DeepAR, NBEATS) so parameters are shared; for extremely many series, variate-as-token models (iTransformer) or parameter-sharing models (DLinear) scale well.
- Covariate-rich tasks: TFT or DeepAR (support known future inputs).
- Real-time inference: prefer convolutional or linear models for minimal latency, or well-optimized sparse-attention Transformers (Informer/TACDformer).

In conclusion, by mid-2026 the field of MTS forecasting in PyTorch is rich and mature: a wide spectrum of architectures, from simple linear regressions to complex Transformer-based networks, all with supporting libraries (PyTorch Forecasting, Darts, NeuralForecast). Recommendation: leverage these new models and tools, but always validate on your domain data.
